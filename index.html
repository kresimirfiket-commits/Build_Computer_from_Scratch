Basic Introduction & Guidelines

Desktop computer

A desktop computer is a type of personal computer designed to be used on a desk or a table. It is a stationary computer system that offers more power and flexibility compared to laptops or mobile devices.

A desktop computer consists of several key components. The central processing unit (CPU) is the brain of the computer, handling most of the calculations and data processing. The motherboard (MOBO) acts as the main circuit board, connecting all the other components and providing power and communication channels between them. The random access memory (RAM) stores data that the CPU can quickly access, while the hard drive or solid-state drive (HDD or SSD) provides long-term storage for files and programs. Other important components include the power supply (PSU), which delivers electricity to the system, and the graphics processing unit (GPU), responsible for rendering images and videos. Peripherals such as the monitor, keyboard, mouse, and speakers are also part of a desktop computer setup.

Desktop computer vs Laptop computer

There are several reasons why you might choose a desktop computer over a laptop. Firstly, desktop computers generally offer more powerful hardware, including faster processors, larger storage capacities, and dedicated graphics cards. This makes them better suited for demanding tasks such as gaming, video editing, or software development. Secondly, desktops are easier to upgrade and customize, allowing you to replace components as needed to keep up with evolving technology.

Building your own desktop computer, also known as a custom personal computer (PC), is a popular option among technical enthusiasts. It allows you to select the specific components based on your needs and preferences, providing a more tailored to one’s computing experience. You can choose CPU, GPU, RAM, storage (SDD and/or HDD), and other components individually, ensuring they meet your desired specifications and budget.

Building your own desktop computer also gives you the freedom to upgrade or replace parts as technology advances.

Desktop computers support a wide range of peripherals. You can connect printers, scanners, external hard drives, webcams, microphones, and other devices to your desktop computer, using universal serial bus (USB), Bluetooth®, or other appropriate connectivity options. This allows you to expand the functionality of your computer and meet your specific needs. And lastly, desktop computers often provide better ergonomics, with larger monitors and full-sized keyboards, which can enhance productivity and comfort for extended periods of use.

Gaming and Programming

Desktop computers are well-suited for gaming, thanks to their powerful hardware capabilities. You can choose components that meet the requirements of the latest games, such as high-performance CPU, GPU, and sufficient RAM. Gaming desktops often have specialized cooling systems to prevent overheating during intense gaming sessions.

Desktop computers are excellent for programming and software development. You can install integrated development environments (IDEs) like Visual Studio, Eclipse, or Xcode to write and debug code. These IDEs provide tools and features that enhance the coding experience, including code auto-completion, debugging tools, and version control integration. Desktop computers offer ample processing power and memory to handle resource-intensive development tasks, compile code quickly and run virtual machines for testing purposes. You can connect multiple monitors to a desktop computer, depending on the available graphics outputs and the capabilities of your graphics card. This can be useful for multitasking, video editing, gaming, programming or other applications where having more screen space is beneficial.

Quick History of PC

The Dawn of Personal Computing (1970s)

The personal computer era began in 1975 with the Altair 8800, a kit-based microcomputer from MITS that captured the imagination of engineers and hobbyists worldwide. Operated through toggle switches with no keyboard or display, it was primitive by any measure — yet it proved that individuals could own and operate a computer. The Altair inspired a young Bill Gates and Paul Allen to write a BASIC interpreter and co-found Microsoft, laying the groundwork for the software industry that would follow.

Apple and the Consumer Market (Late 1970s)

Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs introduced the Apple II in 1977 — a fully assembled machine with color graphics and software support, including the VisiCalc spreadsheet. Alongside Tandy and Commodore, it formed the "1977 Trinity" that transformed the PC from hobbyist curiosity into a practical consumer product. By 1980, personal computers were no longer novelties; they were tools actively sought by homes and small businesses, signaling the start of a genuine mass market.

IBM and the Clone Era (1981–1984)

IBM's 1981 Personal Computer redefined the industry. Built on an open architecture using Intel's 8088 processor and Microsoft's MS-DOS, the design invited cloning. Compaq shipped the first fully compatible clone in 1982, and dozens of manufacturers followed, rapidly commoditizing the PC and cementing the IBM-compatible standard. In 1984, Apple countered with the Macintosh — the first mass-market computer built around a graphical user interface and mouse, introducing a new model of human-computer interaction.

Summary

The personal computer revolution began in 1975 with the Altair 8800. Apple II in 1997 made PCs practical for consumers, while IBM's 1981 open-architecture PC established the dominant industry standard, enabling a wave of compatible clones. Apple's 1984 Macintosh introduced the graphical user interface, a concept Microsoft mass-marketed through Windows 95. The internet then transformed the PC from a productivity tool into a global communications gateway — fundamentally reshaping how humanity works, learns, and connects.

Basic guidelines

If you decided to build custom PC that will be tailored exactly to your needs, here are some key ideas to keep in mind when picking parts.

What is your end goal? Will you be gaming or programming? Which types of games do you want to run, and at what settings? Are you into web development, app development, data science, or any other programming discipline? You could run graphic design software, video editing tools, music production software, and more.

Figure out what your end goal is, choose a budget, and know which components you should invest more money in. You don’t want to spend more money than you have to.

Comparing two components together is not as easy as comparing one specification. All PC parts will have more than one specification that affects the performance of the component at a different weight. Also, PC parts will vary with the features they have. When comparing two components, you may have to create your own sense of importance on certain features.

Components work together and limit each other too. This is called bottlenecking. If you have really fast computer that can run, for example, Minecraft at 500 frames per second, and your monitor can only display at 30 frames per second, you are going to experience only 30 frames per second the monitor is running at. This is a bottleneck where monitor is holding back the power of the computer. Bottlenecks happen throughout different components, so make sure you are not wasting potential of any component of your PC.

Usually the more expensive a component is, the better performance your computer will have. But there is also an effect of diminishing returns. This means that, if you spend twice as much on more expensive part, it doesn’t necessarily mean that you are going to get twice as much performance than you would get if you bought the cheaper part.

This is the hardest part on building your PC since you have to really think about the cost-benefit of each part and each price point. You also have to make sure your parts are all compatible with each other. If they aren’t, then your parts will not work with each other.

To do this, you need to know which parts need to be compatible with each other. They may be electronically incompatible, or physically incompatible due to the size. It is the practice best to check the specifications and compatibility before you buy a part, so explore the manufacturer’s website and find the dimensions, socket type, or port support of your component, and check it against your other component.

Be resourceful. If you have a question, Google it. If you do not understand something, Google it. Read the reviews. Some products are better than others. You don’t want to ignorantly purchase a consistently unreliable part, even if it has the same specifications as another part. Understand where you can cut corners, and where you should shell out more money.

The Windows Era (1985–1995)

Microsoft's Windows platform matured through the late 1980s, achieving mainstream success with Windows 3.1 in 1992. The launch of Windows 95 was a cultural milestone: lines formed outside stores, and over a million copies sold within days. It introduced the iconic Start menu and taskbar, bringing a polished graphical experience to the IBM-compatible world and consolidating Microsoft's dominance as the PC became a fixture in homes and offices across the globe.

The Internet Age and Beyond (1995–Present)

The internet transformed the PC from a productivity device into a global communications hub. Browsers like Netscape Navigator made the World Wide Web accessible to ordinary users, while hardware continued to advance from single-core Pentiums to multi-core processors capable of demanding tasks. Laptops eventually outsold desktops, and smartphones blurred the definition of a personal computer entirely. From the Altair's blinking lights to today's thin, connected ultrabooks, the PC's evolution stands as one of the defining technological journeys of modern history.
